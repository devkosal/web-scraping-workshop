Explanation¶

It is of no surprise that percent salary hike played a huge role in predicting performance score. However, we want to look at the experience features which the R&D head believe are correlated with performance. It turns out that the Head's claims are quite to the contrary. For instance, a higher number of years at the company (YearsAtCompany) was more indicative of a lower performance score.

Meanwhile, total working years had no effect on performance score. Interestingly however, a person's year in current role did make a the second highest impact on performance score. Therefore, those who have been at their current position for a while are likely to be performing well in the company. This could be a sign of problems with firm when it comes to promoting employees to appropriate positions for their skill set.

Returning to the head's point, her beliefs that more experience is tied with performance is not founded based on my analysis.

My Recommendations¶

  • Evaluate the fairness of monthly incomes for employees since a high monthly income leads to stronger attrition

Seeing as the whether someone works overtime or not has the strongest link with attrition, I believe my first recommendation of cutting down overtime will be the most effective. The rapid experiment will take course over the course of a month. We will be evaluating job satisfaction based on overtime.

There will be 100 participants who will not be aware of the experiment's existence (in order to prevent bias). The experiment will be designed in a way where 100 employees who work over time will be selected. All of the individuals not in the control group will no longer be designated to work overtime. There will be 30 individuals in the control group. Two surveys will be performed. One at the start of the experiment and one at the end of the month.

The core foundation of this experiment is past research which links job satisfaction to lower attrition. source

The hypothesis: Lack of overtime leads to higher satisfaction with work

Both the control and experimental group will take both the start and ending surveys. Confounding variables such as age, education, geographical location, etc. will be addressed thought stratified sampling based on these variables. Hence, the experimental and control groups will contain samples of our population which is as consistently represented as possible across the demographic variables mentioned earlier.

The surveys will address questions which relate to overall satisfaction of the employee with their work. Here are some examples of questions / data points we will collect on the survey:

  • How likely are you to recommend working at this firm to a friend on a scale of 1-10?
  • How well are you able to balance your life and work on a scale of 1-10?
  • Has an excess amount of work led you to miss out on your personal activities?
  • How well do you enjoy your work on a scale of 1-10?
  • How excited do you feel coming to work every day in a scale of 1-10?
  • What is the most exciting part of your work day?
  • Can you write about a recent project that you enjoyed working on?

Questions such as these will allow us evaluate the satisfaction rating more in depth. Asking these before and after the experiment will allow us to collect and gauge the employee's sentiment towards work. We will conduct regression on these data points as well between the experimental and control groups to evaluate the effect of no overtime on job satisfaction. Also note that we will perform sentiment analysis on how the participant writes about their recent project and track it over time to see if there is a significant change.
